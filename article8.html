Filmmakers Should Save Old Films

Published in 2026 • Written by Elaidi Maria
Old films are not “old content” — they are cultural archives.

Cinema is more than entertainment — it is a form of art, a cultural memory, and a way for people to understand their time, their society, and even themselves. Yet today, cinema is slowly losing part of its value because many old films are not properly saved, restored, or made accessible to the public.

Without old films, we lose cinema’s evolution

If old films disappear, filmmakers and audiences lose the ability to compare eras and observe how cinema has evolved. Of course, today we have new cameras, new technology, and higher image quality — but what about storytelling, acting, directing styles, and artistic choices from the past?

Many legendary performances and iconic methods of acting risk being forgotten. And without accessible archives, no one can truly evaluate how acting has changed — or whether modern actors are “better” than those who shaped cinema’s history.

Culture should be accessible — not hidden

For anyone working or studying in the film industry, watching both old and new films is essential. It is not only about entertainment, but about building knowledge and culture in the field.

The problem is that many old films are difficult to find on streaming platforms or television. Even movie theaters rarely screen classic films — sometimes only once or twice a year. If that continues, old masterpieces will slowly disappear from public life, and future generations will miss the art and hard work of past actors, filmmakers, and editors.

“Cinema is art. Cinema is culture. If we lose old films, we lose part of our identity.”

Saving old films means saving our artistic heritage. It means protecting stories, performances, and creativity that built the cinema we love today. If we continue to ignore preservation, we risk losing not just movies — but our culture, our memory, and a piece of who we are. Save cinema. Save old films.
